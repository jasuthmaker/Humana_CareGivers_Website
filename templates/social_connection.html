Signs an Elder May Need More Social Support

Social isolation is a health risk equal to smoking 15 cigarettes a day. Recognize the warning signs early.

Declining invitations consistently
Repeated refusals to join family events, calls, or outings they previously enjoyed.
Spending most of the day alone
Limited meaningful interaction with others — not just preference for quiet, but true isolation.
Expressing loneliness or feeling forgotten
Direct statements or indirect hints like "nobody visits anymore" or "I don't matter."
Loss of interest in previously loved activities
Stopped calling friends, attending church, playing cards, or engaging in lifelong hobbies.

Connection FAQ

Start small, even sitting together in silence counts. Presence, not programs, is what matters. Try sharing a cup of tea, watching their favorite show together, or simply asking about their day. Build trust before suggesting structured activities.

Quality over quantity. A 20-minute focused conversation where you're fully present is worth more than an hour of distracted presence. Aim for at least one meaningful interaction daily, even a short call or text. Consistency matters most.

Create a simple shared schedule, even a rotating "who calls grandma on Tuesday" approach works. Group video calls, shared photo albums, and family messaging groups keep elders feeling included without requiring everyone to be physically present.

Absolutely. Senior centers, faith communities, volunteer programs, and adult day programs offer structured social time with peers. Many offer transportation assistance. Even attending once a week can dramatically reduce isolation and provide mental stimulation.
